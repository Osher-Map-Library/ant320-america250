## Alcatraz

Alcatraz Island became a military fort in the 1850s, a military prison was built in 1912 and then, from 1934-1963, it was the infamous prison that most of us today know it for. Before any of this though, the island was an important location for American Indian tribal nations. It was used as a fishing camp and gathering place for the Ohlone and Coastal Miwok tribes. Also, ironically, at times it was used as a sort of prison; a place to send people who broke societal rules within or between these nations. The island had significance to the local American Indian peoples, but, like many places in the United States of America, it was taken and used by the U.S. with no regard for its history and ongoing importance to American Indian peoples. 

After the prison shut down in 1963 there emerged a quickly growing movement from American Indian peoples that eventually included taking back the island. The effort was led by Indigenous activist Richard Oakes. By 1969, a group called Indians of All Tribes (IAT) successfully occupied the island. They cited the 1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie, which stated that all ‘out of use’ federal land was available – in this case, for return to American Indian tribal nations. IAT offered to buy the island back from the federal government for $24 worth of beads and colored cloth, which was a response to the fictional “sale” of Manhattan Island from/by Lenape citizens in 1626. 

On November 20th, 1969, 89 American Indian peoples within IAT set out to occupy the island. There had been previous, smaller attempts but the coast guard had successfully blocked them. The coast guard still, on this attempt, tried to block this attempt but they weren’t able to. On December 10th, IAT leaders met with local officials and, on Dec 24th, there was a press conference talking about the reasons for occupation. At the peak of the occupation there were over 400 peoples of IAT on the island. 

They maintained a school, a day care, a health clinic – all the basic needs a community would need to function. American Indian and non-American Indian peoples would try to bring food and other necessary items to the island. They also sent money and supplies to the “bank of Alcatraz” on the San Francisco side of the water. This “bank” was maintained by Dean Chavers, a member of the Lumbee Tribe. 

The coast guard was increasingly unhappy with the occupation though. They tried, at every turn, to block essential items from getting to the island. The ultimate goal for the occupation wasn’t just to take back Native land; it was to eventually turn the island into a cultural center and museum. IAT planned for this to happen between 1970 and 1971, but they faced resistance from the US government every step of the way. By May 1971 the U.S. federal government shut down electrical power and telephone services on the island and, unfortunately and/or purposely, a fire destroyed some buildings on the island. The occupation was officially over in June when the U.S. federal government removed the remaining people from Alcatraz.

Although the island never fully became an Indigenous cultural site and museum, the IAT movement has had a lasting impact. In the 1970s, many more Indigenous resistance movements developed, including the Wounded Knee Occupation and The Trail of Broken Treaties. These movements were inspired, at least in part, by the occupation of Alcatraz by IAT. This occupation movement sparked serious conversations, for decades to come, about taking back Indian land and reclaiming (or enforcing) Indigenous self-determination. The fact that American Indian peoples were able to occupy the island for over a year, build a sustainable community there, and promote Indigenous autonomies, speaks to the strength of the movement. It is a part of American history that should absolutely be remembered and used to inspire people of all ages.

## Protest and Activism in American History

Acts of protest are protected by the United States Constitution. American citizens have the right to speak, assemble, and petition under the First Amendment of that Constitution. We can be critical of aspects of American society, and we can speak against injustices. Some recent protests include Black Lives Matter, opposition to the Dakota Access Pipeline (NoDAPL) and No Kings. It is important to understand how rights to protest within the United States have been viewed and to recognize that protests will continue to shape American life.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_39" %}

The image “Destruction of Tea at Boston Harbor” depicts colonial Americans throwing tea into the Boston harbor. This is one of the most well-known examples of American protest. Over 100 participants, all white men, dressed as Mohawk peoples to disguise themselves and to avoid charges of treason. During this protest, approximately 340 chests of tea were thrown into the Boston Harbor to protest British tax policies, embodying the idea of “No Taxation Without Representation”. The efforts of this protest eventually led to the creation of the First Continental Congress, and ultimately aided in the creation of the United States as a separate nation.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_38" %}

The Civil Rights movement in the mid 20th century is a well-known and often referenced time of change and activism in American history. Here, we have a flier inviting people to join the March on Washington in 1963. This poster was created by *March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom*. The March on Washington, held on August 28th, 1963, included over 250,000 peaceful protesters, and ended with participants gathered at the Lincoln Memorial to hear Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. recite his “I Have A Dream” speech. Reports indicate that the gathering was neat, orderly, and had very few arrests or incidents with police authorities. This massive protest, which was recognized as the largest demonstration for Civil and Human Rights in the United States at that time, was instrumental in placing pressure onto the Kennedy administration to initiate a strong Civil Rights bill.     

Why haven’t the protests of American Indian peoples been just as impactful within American society? Here, see the “Alcatraz Proclamation”, otherwise known as the “Proclamation to the Great White Father and All His People”. It was read by Richard Oakes to press and government officials at the recently destroyed American Indian Center in San Francisco, 11 days before Oakes led 89 American Indian people on boats to Alcatraz Island, where a 19-month-long occupation and protest began. The Proclamation is written in a style meant to criticize and draw attention to the U.S. Federal Government’s policies toward and regarding American Indian peoples and nations.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_40" %}

At its height, the subsequent takeover of Alcatraz Island included 400 American Indian people and allies. Federal Marshalls forcibly ended the protest held on Alcatraz in June of 1971, but those efforts were not in vain. The occupation of Alcatraz led to federal policy changes – from the end of Indian Termination and Relocation, to a focus on Native American sovereignty. It also aided in the return of 48,000 acres of land and Blue Lake to the Taos tribe. Today, sunrise celebrations are held annually on the island. 

Lately, it has been hard for many young Americans to envision a future that is bright. As such, these protests and examples of activism are a reminder that we must maintain and act upon hope. Dr. Martin Luther Jr., in his “I Have a Dream” speech from 1963, stated:

{% include feature/blockquote.html text="Even though we face the difficulties of today and tomorrow, I still have a dream. It is a dream deeply rooted in the American dream. I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up, live out the true meaning of its creed." %}

I am holding onto this dream, and I wish to inspire other people to consider how life is not set in stone and is able to be changed for the better. The power of protest is the foundation of the United States, and it is within the power of protest that we will continue to see positive and progressive political and social movement.
