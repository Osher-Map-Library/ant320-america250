## Truth in America

The idea for my final came from our visit to the Osher Map Library at University of Southern Maine. It was there that I began to question something I hadn’t ever really thought about before: that maps are created by individuals with their own agendas, views, and perspectives that they can instill in the process of creating a map (Harley, 1989; Wood, 2010). Maps are seen as fact and not to be questioned. But when I realized this, it led me to question who actually decides what is “true” (Crampton, 2001). “A map is a selective representation of reality, not a mirror of it” (Monmonier, 1996).

Over the last 250 years, Americans have developed countless forms of communication, such as maps, newspapers, radio, and now social media, to connect with and understand the world (Briggs & Burke, 2009). This is exactly how perspectives of realities are shaped, even when details and smaller truths are notably left out (Anderson, 2006). Due to this push to create mass communication and idea sharing, “Truth” in America has evolved to the point where it’s been shaped, reshaped, controlled, and, in some cases, manipulated (Foucault, 1980).

Relatedly, a British historian by the name of Edward Carr states that “History is not what happened but what someone *says* happened” (Carr, 1961). In early America, maps were the source of key, high-stakes information. They were to be seen as facts and never questioned. However, this led to the creators of maps having (or pushing) authoritarian perspectives (Harley, 1989). Their maps became authoritarian lenses.

Harley iterates that maps are not neutral because of how they “reproduce the power system that produced them” (Harley, 1989). Maps regarding the Westward Expansion often showed land as ‘available’, public and open for the taking, completely disregarding Indigenous nations already established there (Wood, 2010; Westward Expansion). This led to the inconsistent narrative stating the expansion was for opportunities and progress, while failing to mention realities: displacement and trafficking of Indigenous peoples, theft of Indigenous land and constant conflict.

Americans’ understanding of land, ownership, and power was warped due to their being told (or shown) only part of the truth (Anderson, 2006). “Manifest Destiny” is a clear example of this. Maps and images during this time showed westward expansion – labelled “Manifest Destiny” – as something positive; through use of bright colors, colonial boundary creation, and perpetual assertions that “America” was filled with opportunity (Wood, 2010). These visuals encouraged non-Indigenous settlers to expand and commandeer without completely understanding the effects their movements would have on Indigenous communities and nations. These depictions were harmful, as they did not show the violence and hardship that came with this westward expansion (Harley, 1989). Perspectives – of Indigenous peoples and non-Indigenous peoples – were left out and presented in incomplete ways. This, ultimately, as decades passed, influenced the ways readers of these images understood the nation's development.

America continued to develop, and the ways people received their information were central to that development. News was increasingly spread from radios, newspapers, and social media. Communication platforms evolved in unprecedented ways (Briggs & Burke, 2009), and, ultimately, “the medium is the message” (McLuhan, 1964).

So the question remains: how do we know what is true? In today's world, different news sources present information in different ways that can be differentiated by examining the political identities of their preferred audiences (Pew Research Center, 2020). Social media platforms and economics have contributed to this targeting in news and communication by showing consumers of social media information that is consistent with their beliefs; which seems to create deeper divides in politics and society. I hope that, in the next 250 years, Americans are able to be more aware of how information is created and shared. We need to recognize that the news is not always presenting the full picture. If we can do this, we can begin to think more critically by questioning where our information comes from and considering different perspectives. Although the idea of a single “Truth” is a complicated endeavor, wanting a better understanding of how truths are constructed – especially in media and technology – can help bring us all closer together as a society.
