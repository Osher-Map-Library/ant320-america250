The United States of America is not that old. Someone suggested that we ought to look at it in days. The United States of America (U.S.) is about 90,000 days old. Compare that to the typical (expected) lifetime of a human, which is about 30,000 days. The U.S. is about three full human lives… back to back to back. Not a lot of time, at all!

What is this 90,000 days really about? What is its agenda?

This is an important conversation to have in Maine. “Maine” is not “Vacationland”. It is a constant distraction from the 1980 Maine Indian Claims Settlement Act, which effectively stole 2/3 of Maine’s land/water base from Wabanaki nations. 

As such, this exhibit, which was curated with students in the Spring ’26 semester of ANT 320 (Anthropology and the Museum), attempts to set a new course within future conversations about what the U.S. was, is and ought to be. 

The U.S. began with stolen Indian land. That theft of land hasn’t stopped. About 90,000 days ago, men and women, unable or unwilling to live within English governance, became the nucleus of a movement to create union where there was no union. The U.S. Constitution, published some 4,600 days later, called for a “more perfect union”. 

What were early American leaders working to unionize? They attempted to create unity in the middle of their work to rip the American continent out of the hands of American Indian (Indigenous) peoples and nations. They attempted to create unity within an economy in which peoples from Africa were, increasingly, brought to the U.S. to replace American Indians within chattel slavery.

Large spaces of land and water were turned into prisons or reservations for Indian peoples. “Indiana” is the perfect example of this. The state is named after the term that non-Indian leaders in the U.S. used to denote the place where they attempted to deposit or trash American Indian peoples.

About the time that Abraham Lincoln denoted in the Gettysburg Address that America was “conceived in liberty”, the U.S. solidified itself as the business of converting American Indian worlds and lives into American capital. The Morrill Act of 1862 stole land from American Indian nations and turned it into capital to build universities. The Bear Creek Massacre of 1863 is one of many examples of the U.S. military killing American Indian peoples – in this case, Shoshone people – in the hundreds as part of efforts by American politicians to “settle” the western part of the continent. 

Churches participated in this work. Mormons led the Nephi Massacre of 1853 and the Circleville Massacre of 1866. The Catholic Church, beginning in the 1870s, began to establish a continent-wide system of ethnic cleansing and re-education with money from the U.S. federal government. By the late 1990s, dozens of Catholic priests, each year, were housed within the Boston diocese. For what? For sexually abusing and killing American Indian children within boarding schools and associated churches. Now, in the mid-2020s – 90,000 days into “the United States of America” – Muslim mosques, Jewish synagogues, Baptist churches and other religious organizations are scattered across an America that does not want to confess its original sins. Black Hebrew Israelites –  who were labelled “Black Supremacist” by the Southern Poverty Law Center in 2008– regularly denounce American Indian claims as Indigenous peoples of the U.S. 

In this exhibit, you will witness how the dehumanization of American Indian nations and peoples have led to American theater that tends to keep us from understanding how the U.S. is a makerspace that allows certain people in certain positions of authority/influence to use it (the United States of America) to profit and dominate. In this exhibit, you will witness how the U.S. remains a place where peoples from across the world seek to achieve the “American Dream”. They move to America – their languages, foods and religions in tow – and they set up shop on stolen Indian land. 

How did you get to the United States of America? How has the United States of America oriented and dis-oriented you? How is “America 250” a story of a very small window of time – 90,000 days – within which so much genocide and trauma has occurred? 

These are questions that the curators within this exhibit answer in their own, provocative ways. 

Enjoy.
Think.
Do work to repair.
