## American Monuments

The United States of America portrays westward expansion as beneficial to everyone involved. Monuments such as Mount Rushmore and the Gateway Arch continue to carry and symbolize that narrative. Despite the horrors that westward expansion caused, it has become memorialized in American history as a process of freedom. This process has been hardened within various buildings and statues meant to create an indefinite celebration of America’s theft of the lands of hundreds of American Indian tribal nations.

The story begins like this: since the 1600s, in the name of freedom and democracy, settlers from around the world have traveled west within this continent to an imagined world that, as they believed it to be, was promised to them by God. As they traversed the “unknown”, which was known for thousands of years by American Indian peoples, they were faced with significant resistance from those very same peoples. In their minds, Indigenous (American Indian) peoples were trying to keep them from what God had promised them. In reality, Indigenous peoples were defending their worlds from torture and poisoning. 

Under the flag of “Manifest Destiny”, settlers ceaselessly continued until, ultimately, in 1912, they engulfed Arizona into the American Frontier. The “Wild West” was not inherently wild. Rather, the process of unwavering movement of America’s settlers into land that did not belong to America created conditions within which the land became labelled by American officials as “uncontrolled” and in need of “civilization”. Despite these contradictions between the theater of colonialism and the actual genocidal realities of conquest, westward expansion serves as the keystone and main lever within America’s global message that it (America) is a beacon of freedom and liberty. 

### The Growth of America

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_05" %}

The map created by Esso in 1960 shows the growth of the United States into the country we see today. It features the year in which land was acquired and a few words on how it was acquired; whether it was “annexed”, “purchased”, or “ceded”. On the right side of the map, there is an inset that is titled “Freedom’s Flag Moves Westward”. This feature works to create a national identity; as, no matter the circumstance, it displays a state’s admission to the union as that particular state overcoming a challenge that can only be resolved through assumed unity between states. Notably, on the top of the map, there is a depiction of progression of transportation and industrial advancements –from walking, to trains, to rockets. This not only aligns with the Esso corporate image, it also aligns with Esso’s perspective, as an American corporation, that westward expansion and colonialism are generally positive processes and outcomes.

I implore you to think about how the United States, in its various communities and corporations, has memorialized westward expansion so that we (the viewer) are not allowed to see the full conditions of that expansion.

### Mount Rushmore

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_06" %}

Mount Rushmore was chiseled into the Black Hills, in western South Dakota, between 1927 and 1941. The faces of George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Theodore Roosevelt, and Abraham Lincoln were the subjects of that chiseling. Each president was chosen for a different reason: Washington was a founding father; Jefferson was a leader in expansion; Roosevelt focused on development in the United States; and Lincoln’s reputation is that he preserved the Union. Originally, “Hero’s of the American West” were going to be carved into the mountain. Indigenous peoples such as Sacagawea, Red Cloud, and Crazy Horse were the original options for the monument. However, the designers of the project changed this layout to represent a more patriotic and racially white cast characters at the 150-year anniversary of the United States.

Lakota tribal nations know this mountain as the Six Grandfathers Mountain. It is sacred Lakota land that was stolen by the United States in violation of the 1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie. That theft was, in large part, a result of discovery of gold on the property in 1874. Despite protests by Lakota and non-Lakota people, this relatively small sacred space became the canvas for worship of westward expansion.

Think about the intentionality of carving a monument into sacred Indigenous land. In the image on the bottom right, there is a script: “where living rock eternalizes the nation’s progress.”

Is theft of Indigenous land a form of progress? Do you think that Washington, Jefferson, Lincoln, and Roosevelt represent the first 150 years of American history? Who represents America, today, as we enter “America 250”?

### Devils Tower

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_08" %}

Unlike Mount Rushmore, Devils Tower is a natural phenomenon. It was the first national monument created under the Antiquities Act by Theodore Roosevelt in 1906. The site is sacred to over two dozen American Indian tribal nations. Despite being the first national monument, it will never be treated with the same respect as man-made monuments. Against the wishes of American Indian tribal nations, visitors are able to climb the monument, leaving damage from bolts and climbing equipment. In spite of the constant outcry, the National Parks Service has done little to mediate the situation, but they have implemented a voluntary climbing ban during the month of June, where there are many cultural activities such as the Sun Dance.

Sun Dance is practiced by the Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho nations. During the month of June, Sun Dance is performed for renewal within those communities, and it honors the connection between Earth and the Creator. During this time, individuals (particularly men) would fast from food and water and dance for four days. This ritual was banned by the United States government from 1883 until 1978 as part of a process of forced assimilation of American Indian peoples in the United States of America.

This is a bit ironic. While Alfred Hitchcock was filming the 1959 film *North by Northwest*, he was famously denied access to shoot at Mount Rushmore during the final scene because the National Park Service was worried about the monument being “desecrated”. However, that same sense of guardianship is not deployed by the National Park Service upon calls by American Indian communities to protect Devils Tower.

There have been continuous efforts by American Indian peoples to have the Devils Tower renamed to “Bear Lodge,” which is a more direct translation from Lakota, Crow, Cheyenne, and Kiowa languages. Colonel Richard Dodge documented the name as “Bad Gods Tower” in 1875, leading to the use of this particular name on official maps.

Distinctions in colonial treatment of Indigenous lands, when comparing Mount Rushmore to Devils Tower, reflects a fairly significant pattern of cultural suppression in the United States. Not only are American Indian perspectives of the world devalued at every turn, the National Park Service (as part of the United States federal government) is creating a simplistic version of history that encourages complacency. The version of history that is proposed to us through monuments oftentimes does not include the “full picture” or, at the very least, an invitation for American Indian (Indigenous) peoples to narrate a fuller picture of the world.  Monuments, in the United States, continuously discourage people from learning more about the uncomfortable nature of the history of the United States and policies that have, simultaneously, reaffirmed genocide and justified creation of monuments. 

### Gateway Arch

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_07" %}

The Gateway Arch was completed in St. Louis in 1963. This monument was the centerpiece for the Jefferson National Expansion Memorial, which celebrated the Louisiana Purchase and the importance of St. Louis as the “Gateway to the West”. Beginning at the edge of the Mississippi River, it traces the start of Louis and Clark's Expedition. Now, the monument is a national park dedicated to memorializing manifest destiny-based activities as *ambitious* and *exploratory*. The Gateway Arch does not feature the physical likeness of any one individual. This is ironic because Gateway Arch could not be completed by engineers in the early 1960s. As a result, they were forced to call in Mohawk ironworkers to complete construction of the Gateway Arch. American Indian peoples who had for over 200 years suffered from policies of colonial land theft were, in the end, the only peoples prepared enough and confident enough to do the work of finishing this colonial memorial. And their presences – their images putting the Gateway Arch together – remain largely out of sight.

How does this impact your thinking about westward expansion and interpretation of America’s monuments? Compare the Gateway Arch to Mount Rushmore and Devils Tower: Do you feel that one better represents the colonization of the West than the other? 

### Conclusion

Today, the westward expansion narrative remains stagnant but powerfully present; carved into the Black Hills and welded together in downtown St. Louis. As American Indian (Indigenous) peoples continue to fight against forced assimilations that they faced over centuries, not even an inch is given. In 2020, during the height of de-monument protests in the United States, little attention was paid to the monuments that worship westward expansion and genocidal processes that were (that are) in the foundation of American life. Some monuments are translation errors. Other monuments are colonial graffiti. Other monuments are very obvious resistance to allowing American Indian peoples to be in the middle of our more contemporary American story. If they fail to be repaired – to included and value American Indian (Indigenous) peoples – will anything ever change about the one sided story provided by the United States of westward expansion?

## World's Fairs

The United States has held many celebrations of itself, so much so that there is a fairly common stereotype that American citizens are extraordinarily patriotic. Why are citizens of the United States seen, by the rest of the world, as particularly nationalistic? While no one response can definitively prove or disprove this notion, it might be helpful to use one particular event – the World’s Fairs – to begin to understand why the United States is interpreted as a place of extreme pride and confidence. 

World’s Fairs have not been distinctly “American”. The first organized World’s Fair was in London in 1851, and its original purpose was to highlight an innovative British society. While this first World’s Fair did include inventions from other countries, it tended to cast Britain in an extremely positive light. Britain was depicted as a global leader in areas from technology to culture, and that depiction inspired other countries to follow suit. For example, in 1889, France held a World’s Fair that prioritized its culture. It celebrated the one hundredth anniversary of the French Revolution, which caused many other countries to boycott this particular Fair. Despite it being called a “World's Fair”, it was more like a grand spectacle that showed off France of that time. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_19" %}

The self-love expressed by Britain and France highly motivated America! The Chicago World’s Fair, more commonly known as the “Columbian Exposition”, took place in 1893. It aimed to promote American “culture” as being just as sophisticated as European “culture”.  The centennial World’s Fair, in Philadelphia in 1876, had been successful in presenting America’s industrial might. But organizers of the Columbian Exposition wanted to prove that America's spirit and sense of motivation could rival those of Europe.  Interestingly, a collection of ornate houses, which was at the time called the “White City”, was not designed to represent American architectural styles.  Instead, it celebrated European architectural styles. These houses, also interestingly, were not permanent buildings. They were made up of weak, disposable plaster. This facade - this mirage of Europe built into the Chicago skyline - seemed to mirror America's insecurity. The Chicago World’s Fair was meant to show America’s uniqueness and advancement. However, it seemed to depict America as merely a second Europe…or a lazy copy of Europe. 

Despite this, the Columbian Exposition was imagined as the “American Dream” city. Al Capone’s family moved to Chicago with the belief that it held better opportunities due to its marketing from the Columbian Exposition.  Lyman Frank Baum took inspiration from the “White City” to create the story within his novel The Wizard of Oz. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_18" %}

The Columbian Exposition advertised itself in a way that turned a largely European event into an American classic. Many artists depicted the city as spotless, with crystal-clear water that sparkled in the sun. Chicago in 1893 was, more realistically, unsanitary. With the United States of America still in the Industrial Revolution, the Columbian Exposition dismissed the danger of American life.

*Unsafe working conditions*

*Child labor in factories*

*Continued violence against American Indian (Indigenous) peoples*

*Racial segregation*

The Columbian Exposition’s marketing of the American Dream was one of the greatest examples of American confidence. At the same time, it indirectly demonstrated American insecurity through its use of European history and its presentation of America’s European roots to mask the unpleasant realities occurring day to day in the United States. Ultimately, the Columbian Exposition introduced the American Dream as an inauthentic product that could be sold anywhere and to anyone.

The United States hosted other World’s Fairs in 1901 in Buffalo, New York, 1904 in Saint Louis, Missouri, 1939 in New York City, and again, in 1965 in New York City. The 1901 and 1904 World’s Fairs are important because they showcased technological advancements and presented America as a leading technological power. The two New York City World’s Fairs were a bit different.  Their roles seemed to be to uplift and distract American citizens from doubt.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_21" %}

The 1939 World’s Fair in New York City was a grand distraction from World War II and the Great Depression. It was crafted in such a way that it showed American Isolation as necessary – as opposed to earlier American World’s Fairs that were designed to connect Europe to America. Indeed, the 1939 World’s Fair was a detachment from Europe. It was focused on the civic progression of America. For example, it contained different streets and buildings that corresponded to influential political figures or aspects of the American Constitution.

Given the current political climate in the United States, “America 250” seems like the New York City World’s Fair of 1939. There was a heavy focus on American patriotism, which included messages of peace and freedom. Ironically for its time, the United States was heavily invested and profiting from actively supplying the war in Europe for the Allies. Today, “America 250” is shrouded by America’s militarized activity in Iran, Venezuela, and other areas of the world.

“America 250” is also overshadowed by undeniable economic suffering. In the Covid 19 pandemic, there were comparable rates of poverty and other economic problems in some years comparable to those of the Great Depression, for somewhat brief periods of time. Today, in 2026, in the aftermath of the Iran war, energy prices are at the center of inflationary activity that seems unprecedented.

In 1965, the message of the World’s Fair in New York City did not change too much. World War II had concluded, and America was using the World’s Fair to establish dominance over the Soviet Union through a display of technological superiority. This Fair highlighted America's need for recognition, along with its realization that with every new invention - like the television –corporations and other groups could “advertise the good life” and create new forms of escapism and grand distraction. This came across as confidence and/or dominance.

See the Shell map, for example. This is a gas company that, at that moment in 1965, was promoting the World's Fair as something that was both accessible and necessary to enjoy the American Dream. Shell advocated for the visitor to travel in a car, which ideally allowed for more people to go and see the Fair. Shell gasoline fueled those cars. Through this coaxing, Shell profited. But on a larger scale, government and corporate leaders in the United States wanted to establish America as the leading world power, willing to project as much as it needed to –and prove whatever was necessary –to continue the comfort of American prosperity and the illusion of being a world leader and “number one”.  What has been done in the past is important to the future. How the United States has been presented within America’s World’s Fairs has been hollow, and its presentations have created a reputation of greatness, but in reality, never delivered on all its promises.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_20" %}

If there is a future World’s Fair in the United States, it ought to depict the America of today as it truly is, so that future generations looking back (and other onlookers experiencing it in the moment) will not be misinformed or fall for the illusion of self-aggrandizing American greatness. Celebrating one's own “culture” is something that should be done; it is not a sin, nor should it be. But the problem is dishonesty. It is nearly impossible to learn from a history that betrays itself for vanity’s sake. As Americans, it will be fascinating to see if upcoming World’s Fairs will break the trend.
