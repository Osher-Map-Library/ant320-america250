## What does being American mean to you?

The United States of America, it seems, is dividing. We have grown politically and economically on the world stage due to our large, diverse geography. At the same time, increasing disruption across the nation is the effect of a fading national identity and distrust in the current political system that governs American life. As the internet age has brought Americans closer together --melding different trends, cultures, and ideas -- our country has continued to become polarized and separate. 

With a country comparable in square mileage to the entire continent of Europe, how has the United States of America stayed unified for so long? 

In order for this country to withstand another 250 years of existence, we will need to overcome corruption, encourage reparations, and pick a symbol of unification. The US has never entirely been a land of freedom, but in order to continue as a whole, we need to figure out how we will continue to tell the story of American unification. 

What does American unification look like? How do we see it? If we look at particular maps created over the last century, the crafting of unification often seems captured in individual visions. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_25" %}

Consider the first map. It was published by the General Drafting Company in 1960 and created for Esso. Esso created this map to endorse continental travel via car in order to sell more gas. This map features famous lore stories such as Blackbeard and Paul Bunyan as well as lesser-known figures such as Sam McGee. Some of the figures are fictional, while some actually existed. 

As you look through the map, find states that are important to you and see if you’ve ever heard of the folklore in the state. *Do you think this map shows stories that you heard before? Are these stories a story of the entire nation, or just that state or small region?* 

Consider a second map. Released in 1932 and made by Louis Fancher, this pictorial map shows the location of foods produced in each state of the US. The Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co. was the publisher of this map. The pictures of the corners of the map show how much poultry, meat, fish, and grains come from different regions. Each state has illustrations of the foods produced there (in that particular state) and a list of foods in the middle of each state.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_24" %}

Because of the United States’ vast and varying geography, we are able to grow and cultivate much of our food within the nation. Even today, in an age of extreme globalization, most foods that Americans consume are from within the United States. In 2016, there was a study conducted by the USDA to see what percentage of food and beverage purchases are American goods. 82.6% of all food and beverage purchased in the United States was from the United States (USDA, 2019).

*Think of a meal that you commonly eat. What states produce the ingredients of that meal? What are the people in those states possibly eating from your state? What do you think you have in common with them?*

Consider a third map. This map, which depicts products produced by each state and the trucks that carry them, was published by the Automobile Manufacturers Association in 1958. Around the border of the map of the United States, there are small illustrations of trucks and details about them. *Notice something: many of the statements imply how these trucks assist the country*. For example: “carries 80% of the nation's livestock from farms to stockyards” or “nine out of ten motor vehicles shipped by highway”. These statements imply the role of the shipping industry in keeping the nation together (e.g., through supply chains), and the role of shipping elements (e.g., trucks) in the maintenance of order within American life.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_22" %}

The groundwork for a cohesive highway system came about within an Act passed by Congress in 1956 called the Federal-Aid Highway (FAH) Act. This map was only released two years after the FAH Act was passed. The FAH Act became the foundation for policies that propelled building interstates across the country through a mandate to connect all cities with a population over 100,000. 

By 1985, over 630 billion ton-miles of goods were sent around the country (National Museum of American History, 2024). A ton-mile is one ton of material carried across one mile. What was transported in 1985 was four times as many ton-miles as what was transported in all of the 1950s. A country once separated by mud roads, railroads, and difficult terrain, became, by 1985, intricately connected through roads that allowed any automobile to go practically anywhere. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_31" %}

*The Conquest of a Continent* was published in 1933 by the American Association of University Women and created by August Kaiser and Agnes Larson. Their map was published the same year as a book with the same title. That book, written by Madison Grant, was a eugenicist narrative that made European (white) movement into America the beginning of history. It dismissed American Indian (Indigenous) peoples through the strongly shared idea that American Indian peoples were a threat to white civilization. Similar to that book, Kaiser’s and Larson’s map tells the story of an America that was waiting for Europeans to move in and take over the continent. It identifies “white settlements”. It describes Robert E. Lee, who defended slavery in the Confederacy, as a “great solider and a gentleman”. It describes the “last stand” of “the Apaches”. 

The border of the map frames the story of America with events – from 1492 to 1919 – that established white ownership of the United States. The first frame reads: “Europe starts us off, 1492-1620”. Hundreds of American Indian nations engineered the continent before and after 1492. Those American Indian nations still exist. Why are their fullness of presences, all over the continent, limited to images that make it seem like American Indian peoples are disappearing?

A study done by Public Religion Research Institute shows that, in 2022, only 4% of the Americans polled stated that being of European heritage is very important to being truly American (Stewart, et. al, 2022). Why, almost a century earlier, did books and maps attempt to unify America through a celebration of European roots? 
	
Explore the proclamations of this map: 

“We become a world power…”

“We decide the West shall be free forever…”

The use of “we” is important. Almost every statement that is included in this map states that the US is a “we”. *What does “we” mean to you? When you view this map, what makes you feel a part of the “we”?*

## Puerto Rico and U.S. territories

Puerto Rico is officially a “U.S. territory”. Its current events – news of what happens within Puerto Rico – only becomes news in the rest of the United States of America when a major hurricane devastates this island nation. Anything else is insignificant.  

Puerto Rico uses US currency. Its citizens are subject to federal laws, but they do not have a voting representative in Congress. This means that though Puerto Rico is underneath US jurisdiction, the people who live there do not have a say in making the laws that control them. Puerto Rico, additionally, has been expensively and forcefully kept as a US territory to maintain military control in the Caribbean Sea. 

Over a thousand years before the Spanish colonized parts of the Caribbean in 1492, Puerto Rico was home to Arawak and Taino peoples. It was colonized, initially, by gold-hungry, slaveholding, slaughterous colonizers. Puerto Rico, centuries later, became a U.S. territory in 1898 after the Spanish-American War. 

Amílcar Barreto, a professor at Northeastern University, a Puerto Rican native, and authoritative scholar on the topic, argues that: “Statehood in the U.S. was not about absorbing new people but about reabsorbing Americans who migrated West.”(Howell) Oregon. California. Alaska. Hawaii. America’s colonial fingers were already manipulating life in those spaces and places before, ultimately, they became official “states”.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_36" %}

The story might be different for Puerto Rico. It is often treated, not as place of Americans, but as a place that Americans need. Discriminatory policing of religion, language, and culture – and concerns about race and representation - keeps the United States of America from integrating PR as a state. Instead, the island is held hostage in a state of limbo under the status of “territory”. 

Puerto Rico formed its own government in 1900, just not a sovereign one. The Center for Constitutional Rights writes about a series of Supreme Court rulings regarding Puerto Rico’s independence: “the Court reasoned that Puerto Rico is ‘foreign to the United States in a domestic sense, because the island had not been incorporated into the country.” (Martinez). This is a debilitating cycle of inaction and possession. At times, hope seems to arise. For example, through the Foraker Act of 1952, Puerto Rico became a commonwealth, which gave its citizens greater autonomy over internal affairs. However,  full representation in Washington, D.C. (in U.S. Federal governance) was denied. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_37" %}

In the 1990s, the Congress of the United States played with a handful of bills regarding Puerto Rico’s independence. Twelve more similar bills popped up between 2000 and 2024. Nothing happened. 

The administration of Donald Trump, in 2017, blocked $20 billion in hurricane relief for Puerto Rico following the devastating aftermath of Hurricane Maria. This natural disaster contained a massive death toll of 2,975 people. It triggered the second longest electricity blackout in world history. During his presidency, Joe Biden rolled back some of Donald Trump’s Puerto Rican-specific restrictions and began sending relief aid. However, less than one-third (less than 33%) of that aid actually reached communities in Puerto Rico. 	

The citizens of Puerto Rico have shifted positions on statehood. In 1967, 1993 and 1998, they voted down referendums advocating for statehood. However, in 2012, 2017 and 2020, a voting majority showed strong support for becoming the 51st state within the United States of America. These were non-binding referendums (that is, they had no real power to change Puerto Rico’s status), but they marked a major shift in popular opinion. 

What does this view of “U.S. territory” show us about the nature of America’s agenda? There are four other U.S. territories: Guam, U.S. Virgin Islands, Samoa, and Mariana Islands. In all cases, expect Guam, their citizens are also citizens of the United States. If they are citizens, why aren’t their lands “states”? With statehood comes power within the federalist system. The state is able to defend itself through “state’s rights”. It is able to appeal for permanent federal resources as a state. Extension of statehood, it seems, threatens the power of the America as a national and federal enterprise.
