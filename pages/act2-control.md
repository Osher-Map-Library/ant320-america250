## Boxing in of Native land

When you look at maps of American Indian (Native American) land from different time periods side by side, a clear pattern begins to appear. The loss of Native land in the United States was not random. It happened over time because of specific government laws and policies. The Indian Removal Act of 1830 was passed by the U.S. government to move American Indian tribal nations from their homelands in the southeastern United States to land west of the Mississippi River. While it was presented as a way to reduce conflict and open land for settlement, it was actually a forced removal.

Thousands of American Indians were made to leave their homes, often with very little time to prepare. This included the Trail of Tears, during which many American Indian peoples died due to disease, starvation, sexual assault and harsh travel conditions. When you compare maps from before and after this 1830 Act, the change is immediate. Native land in the East disappears and is replaced by smaller areas in the West that are labelled in proximity to American Indian peoples. These maps, side-by-side, illustrate how quickly and forcefully policies focused on stolen Indian land reshaped the country (Encyclopaedia Britannica).

After Indian Removal, in the early 1830s, the U.S. government continued to steal land through treaties presented and signed under duress. Treaties were supposed to be agreements between Native nations and the U.S. Federal government, but they were often unfair. Many American Indian nations were pressured into signing them, or did not fully benefit from the agreements. Maps that show treaty boundaries, like those from the Smithsonian’s Nation to Nation project and the Library of Congress, make this process easy to see. Each new treaty reduced the amount of land that American Indian nations controlled. Instead of one single event, land loss happened over many steps, and over long time. Large and connected territories became smaller and more separated. These maps illustrate how legal agreements through treaties signed under pressures of American violence were used to slowly take land and turn it into fixed colonial territories.

This is illustrated within the history of the Dawes Act of 1887, which was also known as the General Allotment Act. This law changed how land was owned. Instead of land being held by American Indian tribal nations, as a whole, it was divided into individual plots and given to Native families and individuals. The goal of this Act was to force American Indian peoples to adopt European-American ways of surveying, controlling and stripping ecological and agricultural landscapes. After Indian-owned land was divided, any leftover land (called “surplus”) was sold to non-American Indian settlers.

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_17" width="50" %}

This caused a huge loss of Native land, which becomes boxed or squared. Maps from this time, including the 1908 map of Tripp County, South Dakota, clearly show this squaring of land. Land is divided into small, square sections, eventually forming a grid pattern. This geometrical alignment sits harshly juxtaposed to earlier maps of the American landscape (that show very few signs of this squaring) and illustrates the civil domination of land as it was ushered from American Indian ownership to the coffers of private and public land trusts and owners (National Archives; Osher Map Library). (This is where and when the discipline of “civil engineering” began to become important in the United States.)

The Dawes Act replaced Indigenous land engineering and ownership with a system that focused on land measurement and intense accounting-based control of land. Reservation maps from the late 1800s often have straight, box-like borders that ignore natural features like rivers and ecosystems. This illustrates how land was increasingly divided based on colonial government systems that aimed to box Indigenous peoples into (or out of) place, and not on how Indigenous peoples lived or understood the environment as part of the worldviews of their tribal nations. As such, maps became a way to enforce colonial authoritarian land theft. Maps were not merely recordings of that theft.

Records from the National Archives illustrate how the United Stated Federal government measured, surveyed, and officially recorded land divisions. Meanwhile, recent research helps confirm that this process was planned and systematic. Studies using GIS technology –like those focusing on land at Standing Rock –illustrate how American Indian land was divided into smaller and more disrupted pieces over time. These studies verify what we otherwise see in historical maps: the fact that American Indian land loss was not accidental or incidental, but carefully organized through laws and policies (MDPI, 2021).

{% include feature/image.html objectid="ant320_16" width="75" %}

When all of these maps and sources are observed and analyzed together, they tell a clear and powerful story. American Indian land began as large and connected, but over time it became smaller, divided, and narcissistically controlled. Laws like the Indian Removal Act and the Dawes Act, along with treaties that were created in conditions of violence and duress, played a major role in this change. These policies did more than take land.  They affected how American Indian peoples lived, practiced their ceremonies and politics, and connected to their communities and sacred places/spaces.

This history is still important today. Many reservations are limited in size and resources because of the actions of squaring and controlling land. As such, these maps show more than changes in land. They let us see and understand long-term impact of government actions focused on land theft and suppression of American Indian peoples and nations. By examining the background of these policies – and seeing how they appear on maps – it becomes easier to understand how present-day landscapes carry the undeniable mark of programs of genocide in the United States.
